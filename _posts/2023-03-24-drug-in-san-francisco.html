San Francisco has been dealing with a serious drug problem for many years, having more drug crimes and overdose deaths than many other US cities. The city’s history of drug use, boosted by the opioid epidemic which has swept across the United States over the past few decades, has caused this to be a problem that needed dealing with.

San Francisco has implemented a variety of strategies to try and combat this drug crisis, such as expanding access to addiction treatment and recovery services, increasing access to overdose prevention tools, and implementing harm reduction strategies like needle exchange programs [1].

In this article, we have analyzed a dataset containing crimes within San Francisco from the years 2003 to 2017. We will focus on drug crimes occurring in the years 2010-2017. We will be analyzing the effects of the strategies San Francisco have implemented to combat the drug crisis.

Lower number of reports means less overdose deaths?

If the drug-crisis was directly related to the number of drug-related arrests occurring per year, then this would be great progress. However, one thing that this graphic doesn’t show is the number of overdose deaths. According to the San Francisco Department of Public Health, the number of overdose deaths per year increased from 104 to 369 between 2010 to 2017. This has caused some skepticism of San Francisco’s strategy, with some saying this “progressive” strategy is killing hundreds of people [3].
