Welcome

The universe is made of stories. Nowadays, the most impactful stories come in the form of movies. These stories are a reflection of society and show our norms and values. So what do we learn from movies? In this datastory, we take you on a journey through time and space and discover movies through the eyes of characters. 

Data

For this story, we will be using the CMU movie summary corpus. What does this dataset entail?

450,669 characters

81,741 movies

42,306 plot summaries

Additionally, a there are 500 characters that are classified into one of 72 tv trope clusters.

Genres

Let us start our journey by looking at different movie genres. Can you guess how many different genres there are?

Number of genres

364

The figure below shows the prevalence of different genres across time. One can see that the movie industry has changed significantly over time. Up until 1920, black-and-white, silent films and short films were dominant. The first movie with both synchronized music and speech is The Jazz Singer. This marked the end of the era of silent movies, as movies with sound ("talkies") soared in popularity from this point on. What pattern do you see for your favorite movie genre? 

Movie characters

We now move onto studying character archetypes. Humans are complex and diverse, and no two people are the same. Yet we tend to find the same character types again and again in movies. Who does not know the heroic protagonist in action movies, or the funny sidekick in action comedy. TV Tropes is a rich database that, among others, describes a multitude of character archetypes. The CMU movie summary corpus has classified 500 characters based on the TV Tropes clusters, which provides a first insight into the diversity of the dataset. 

What are the top 5 clusters for romance movies you think? How about action movies?

These tropes give a first glance at the different types of characters, but use only a small share of our data. To obtain a more comprehensive view on all character archetypes, we should consider the whole dataset. Therefore, by using a coreNLP pipeline in combination with DBScan clustering, we clustered all characters. So grab some popcorn, as we dive into the clusters.

Character clustering

Before we dive into the nitty-gritty details, take some time to explore the clusters! Can you find your favorite movies? Your favorite characters?

All done playing? So how did we get these clusters. This process starts with processing all plot summaries, using the coreNLP pipeline. INSERT TEXT MARGUERITE OR ANTOINE. 

Once we obtained the descriptions for all characters, the hard work was done. We embedded the descriptions using spaCy, and weighted them by category. The character titles and attributes gained more weight, whereas the verbs were weighted less. We then first reduced the dimensionality of the embedding to 50 dimensions by applying principal component analysis. Next, the dimensionality is brought down to 3 by means of t-SNE dimensionality reduction. Last, we applied DBSCAN clustering to obtain the clusters, which we labeled manually based on the descriptions of the characters. Specifically, we looked at the ten characters that originated from movies with the highest box office revenue in that cluster.

Let us already note one finding from these clusters. The first thing that strikes the eye is the big central cluster, which consists of Heroes and anti-heroes. This is not so surprising, as a hero or anti-hero is usually the main character of a movie. Naturally, many of the characters that were extracted from the plot summary are main characters.

Cluster analysis

We now analyze the clusters across two dimensions: time and the box office revenue. The graph on the right hand side might seem daunting at first but no worries: let's run through it step by step.

For this figure we have selected the ten biggest clusters, one can toggle between clusters with the drop-down menu at the top left corner. The graph then displays the percentage of characters that belong to the cluster for each time period. If the bars increase across time for a cluster, this indicates that the cluster has become more prevalent in movies during the last century. Additionally, the color of a bar chart indicates the percentage of revenue created by the movies to which these characters belong.
